# GOAL Sales Training: Setting Firm Expectations & Handling Objections

**Training Module Outline**
**Audience:** New and existing GOAL Sales Representatives
**Duration:** 90-120 minutes (includes role-play exercises)
**Objective:** Equip reps to set non-negotiable expectations during the sales process so the Account Management team can succeed post-sale.

---

## Pre-Training Context (5 minutes)

### Why This Training Exists

- Successful GOAL accounts share one thing in common: the rep set strict boundaries during the sale
- Failed accounts almost always trace back to expectations that were never set or were softened to close the deal
- As Chris Nashed warns: *"It's not going to be surprising to me if some sellers who feel like they can get somebody over the finish line... are going to talk sunshine, rainbows, and lollipops."*
- **The cost of a bad close:** When reps skip the hard truths, Account Managers become the "bearer of bad news," trust erodes, and the account churns — costing everyone revenue

---

## Part 1: The Data Requirement (Mandatory) — 30 minutes

### 1.1 The Core Principle

GOAL is a data-driven platform. Without backend disposition data, the Account Manager cannot optimize campaigns, prove ROI, or lower the client's CPA. Providing this data is **not optional** — it is a requirement of partnership.

**What we need from every client:**
| Data Point | Required? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Phone number | **YES** | The unique identifier — this is how GOAL maps everything |
| Quoted (yes/no) | **YES** | Did the agent quote the prospect? |
| Sold (yes/no) | **YES** | Did the agent bind the policy? |
| Premium amount | Nice to have | Enables premium-based ROI reporting |
| Name/Address/PII | **NO** | Explicitly tell prospects we don't need or want this |

### 1.2 Talk Track: The "Flying Blind" Analogy

This is the single most important analogy reps must internalize. It frames the data requirement as being *in the prospect's best interest*, not a burden.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"Everything we do here is a data-driven decision. In order for me to send you better leads, I have to know who you contact, who you quote, and who you sell. Without it, I'm flying completely blind."* — Nick Stamboulis
- *"Without that data, you get half the puzzle piece... it's a recipe for disaster."* — Alex Roy
- *"The key thing with us, in order for us to really help you, is we need disposition data. I got to know who you're quoting, who you're selling to optimize the campaigns. Otherwise, we're just kind of flying blind for you."* — Chris Nashed

### 1.3 Talk Track: The "Bare Minimum" Technique

Prospects often hesitate because they assume the data requirement is complex, invasive, or time-consuming. Immediately lower the barrier.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"All we really care about is the phone number. I don't care what their name is or address, any of that stuff. I need phone number. I need to know if you quoted it and if you sold it. That's it."* — Chris Nashed
- *"I don't even give a s*** what their name is. What we need and what the system uses as a unique identifier is the phone number of the prospect."* — Chris Nashed (internal training)

### 1.4 Talk Track: Explaining the Phone Number as Unique Identifier

Prospects need to understand *how* the system works so the request makes sense. Walk them through the mechanics briefly.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"We use the phone number as a unique identifier for the consumer. Whether you quoted it, whether you sold it — I can map that in. Then I can tell you by ad group: how many quotes, what's my quote conversion rate from a click, cost per quote, how many households did I sell..."* — Chris Nashed

### 1.5 Talk Track: Overcoming the "Sorting" Objection

Prospects will push back assuming they need to manually filter their CRM to isolate only GOAL leads. Kill this objection immediately.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"Most agents we work with will just send us that export out of lead manager with all of their stuff. They don't even filter by vendors. Give me all the stuff you got. Our system is smart enough to map things based off of that unique identifier, which is the phone number. If it didn't originate from GOAL, it doesn't count it for anything."* — Chris Nashed
- *"Realistically, every time we meet, most people just send a 90-day rolling raw data set, and the day before we meet, I get it over, by the time we meet it's uploaded, and all your data looks beautiful."* — Alex Roy

### 1.6 Establishing the Non-Negotiable Boundary

Reps must not soften this requirement. If the prospect won't commit to providing data, they are not a good fit for GOAL.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"You've got to give us the data that we need so that we can do our jobs. That's paramount. If we don't have data, we can't do our job. And we can't help you."* — Chris Nashed
- *"Go buy leads from anybody else at that point if you're not going to provide us the disposition data we need to actually optimize your campaigns. Otherwise, I don't know what the hell you're paying us to do."* — Chris Nashed (internal training)

### 1.7 Real-World Failure Example: What Happens Without Data

**Case Study — Alex's Early Accounts:**
During a training review, Chris highlighted a period where Alex was conducting account reviews without requiring agents to provide their data. Without disposition data, Alex was forced to discuss only "surface level metrics" like cost per click or lead volume.

Chris's assessment: *"What do you possibly be talking about for 45 minutes... without any data to understand what they were doing? And then they would churn out, and I'm like... how do we know anything?"*

**Takeaway:** If the rep doesn't set this expectation during the sale, the AM inherits an unmanageable account that will churn.

## Part 2: The 90-Day Ramp — 30 minutes

### 2.1 The Core Principle

GOAL is not an overnight solution. Digital marketing campaigns require data to mature, algorithms to optimize, and sales cycles to complete. Reps must secure a verbal 90-day commitment from every prospect before closing.

**Why 90 days?**
- Auto leads typically take **60 days** to show full maturity
- Home insurance leads require a **60-90 day window**
- Some leads close on the first call; others need time to "bake and mature" through the pipeline
- The Account Manager needs enough data points to make meaningful optimization decisions

### 2.2 Talk Track: The "No Silver Bullet" Disclaimer

Proactively defuse unrealistic expectations with clear, memorable language.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"This is not like I push a button and magical things like money is going to fall out of the sky, right? It does take some time. And so I typically ask that you give us at least a 90-day window to properly optimize the campaigns."* — Chris Nashed
- *"This is not like a one and done. I'm not going to get you, like, make it rain for you on the first day."* — Chris Nashed
- *"We're going to try to make it rain the first month for you, but I don't want to set improper expectations there."* — Chris Nashed
- *"This is not a magic bullet. This is not a golden ticket to write millions of dollars in premium. It can be, but it still takes a lot of work."* — Chris Nashed

### 2.3 Talk Track: Positioning the 90 Days as "Data Maturity"

Frame the 90 days as a *technical requirement*, not a stall tactic. Explain the "why" so the prospect buys into the logic.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"The only thing I ask is that you just give us like three months. It takes time for us to dial this thing in. And so we usually know within 60 to 90 days if it's something that is going to work for your business."* — Chris Nashed

**Key concept to communicate:**
- Month 1 = Learning phase. Collecting data, testing audiences, establishing baselines
- Month 2 = Optimization phase. Adjusting campaigns based on real disposition data
- Month 3 = Performance phase. Campaigns are dialed in, ROI becomes measurable

### 2.4 Talk Track: The "Handshake Agreement" Technique

This is one of the most effective closes in the GOAL playbook. Contrast GOAL's no-contract flexibility with the rigid commitments of traditional vendors, then immediately pivot to a verbal 90-day ask.

**The formula:**
1. Remove fear: *"No hidden costs, no contracts. I'm not going to lock you into anything."*
2. Pivot to the ask: *"All I ask is that if you work with us, give us 90 days to work through what we need to work through to better optimize the campaigns."*

**Validated example — Mayra Menchaca demo:**
- Rep: *"No contracts... We asked for three months at a minimum. It's informal. It's like a handshake agreement. I don't need to make you sign something. It's just kind of like, give me three months."*
- Prospect's response: *"One month is not enough data. It's just not enough."*
- **Result:** The prospect voluntarily agreed because the logic was sound.

### 2.5 Reinforcement: The Onboarding Call Script

The 90-day expectation is set during the sale and **reinforced on the first onboarding call**. The Onboarding Call Guide provides this exact phrasing for Account Managers:

> *"I want to be upfront about something. The first 30-60 days are about learning, not judging. We're going to collect data on what's working in your market, then optimize from there. The clients who do best treat this as a 90-day ramp, not a week-one verdict."*

Reps should tell prospects during the demo: *"When you get on your onboarding call, your Account Manager is going to reiterate this same thing. We're all on the same page."*

### 2.6 Real-World Failure Example: The Kansas City Account

**Case Study — Premature Churn:**
Alex shared an example of a Kansas City agent who launched with a $25/day budget. On day one, he received one inbound call, quoted it, and was thrilled. When volume naturally dropped the following week (because $25/day yields minimal volume), his mindset shifted entirely.

Alex's account: *"He churned like in two weeks. I think it was 11 days or 13 days... he gave us seven or nine business days and saw some results but not enough to continue."*

Alex's reflection: *"I feel like maybe I could have said like $25 a day is going to get you very little results... if you double down on the 90-day thing... you're going to get people like that."*

**Takeaway:** If the rep doesn't explicitly secure the 90-day commitment, low-budget clients will judge the platform on a few days of data and churn before the AM has any chance to optimize.

## Part 3: Handling the "Burned by Lead Vendors" Objection — 30 minutes

### 3.1 The Core Principle

This is the single most common objection reps will face. Nearly every insurance agent has spent money on shared leads from aggregators and had a terrible experience. Reps must **validate the frustration**, **explain why the experience was bad**, and **reframe GOAL as a fundamentally different model**.

### 3.2 Talk Track: The "Anti-Lead Company" Rebuttal

Step 1 is to immediately separate GOAL from whatever the prospect is comparing it to.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"I don't want you to think of us as like another lead company because we're not a lead company. We're a marketing platform."* — Chris Nashed
- Explain that GOAL enables agents to *"cut out the middleman"* and advertise programmatically on the same networks as major carriers (Progressive, Geico) and aggregators (EverQuote, Smart Financial)
- Position the agent at the *"top of the funnel"* — they become their own marketing company

### 3.3 Talk Track: Exposing the Aggregator Model (The "Dirty Game")

Validate the prospect's pain by explaining *exactly* why their past experience was bad. When the prospect hears you describe their exact pain point, trust skyrockets.

**The aggregator model explained (use this framework):**

1. **Consumer fills out one form** on EverQuote, Smart Financial, etc.
2. **The aggregator sells that data 3-8+ times** to different agents
3. **The consumer gets bombarded** with calls from agents they've never heard of
4. **By the time Agent #4 calls**, the consumer is annoyed, unresponsive, or already quoted
5. **The agent blames the "leads"** when the real problem is the model

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"That's the reason why the lead gen game is a dirty game, right? It dilutes the quality of that opportunity, and it bombards that consumer with a crap ton of phone calls when they maybe only submitted their information one or two times."* — Chris Nashed
- *"Traditional vendors operate by trying to monetize that click and that consumer as many times as humanly possible."* — Chris Nashed
- *"When you're buying shared leads, you're just buying a name and a phone number. The consumer has zero visibility into you and your agency. They don't know who you are."* — Chris Nashed

### 3.4 Talk Track: Pitching the 1-to-1 Model

After exposing the aggregator model, contrast it with GOAL's 1-to-1 approach. Hit three key differentiators:

**Differentiator 1: Direct Consent**
- *"You're collecting that consent one-to-one. I'm not selling that data three, four, five, six, seven, eight, nine times like the other guys do."* — Chris Nashed
- The consumer sees the agent's branded ad, clicks it, and submits their information directly

**Differentiator 2: Brand Equity**
- *"Leveraging GOAL is a means not only to generate your own leads but also to build your brand. Create visibility for your agency."* — Chris Nashed
- Traditional leads build zero brand awareness; GOAL ads build the agent's name in their market

**Differentiator 3: The "Final Destination"**
- Traditional vendor forms show *more* competing ads at the end to keep the consumer shopping
- GOAL's QuoteFunnel creates a *"final destination"* where the *"only call to action that we present to a consumer is to call you"*
- Result: roughly **1 out of 10 leads call the agency directly** as inbound calls

### 3.5 Talk Track: The "Seeing Jesus" Analogy (Handling the "Exclusive" Question)

Prospects will ask: "Are these exclusive leads?" This is a trap. Never promise exclusivity — instead, use radical transparency to build trust.

**Key phrases to use:**
- *"Exclusive in this space — you've got a better chance of seeing Jesus than generating real, true exclusivity. And what I mean by that is we can't control human behavior."* — Chris Nashed
- *"Anyone that tells you you're going to get exclusive in the lead gen game is lying through their freaking teeth, and you should fire them immediately. Because that means they're saying that they can control human behavior."* — Chris Nashed
- **Then pivot:** Explain that while no one can guarantee a consumer won't fill out multiple forms, GOAL is the *closest thing to exclusivity* because the data is never resold by GOAL

### 3.6 Real-World Failure Example: The "Lead Vendor Mindset" Client

**Case Study — The Text-Only Complainer:**
An account came in treating GOAL like a generic lead vendor. He complained about individual leads, demanded constant adjustments, but refused to ever get on a call with his Account Manager.

Tom's account: *"He refuses to meet. He refuses to ever meet with Nick. He's never met with anyone. He'll only do it over text."*

To appease the complaints, Nick began manually removing publisher sources entirely instead of properly filtering — which Chris flagged as a destructive approach to campaign management.

Tom's conclusion: *"If you don't ever want to meet or focus on optimizing this, maybe we're not the right product for you."*

Alex's reflection: *"They're so used to like, oh, yeah, I'm with a new lead vendor. Versus like, no, you're the marketing company now."*

**Takeaway:** When the rep fails to reframe GOAL away from "lead vendor," the client brings toxic lead-buying habits into the relationship and resists the collaborative partnership model GOAL requires.

## Part 4: Interactive Exercises — 20-30 minutes

### Exercise 1: Role-Play — Setting the Data Requirement (10 min)

**Setup:** Pair reps. One plays the prospect, one plays the GOAL rep.

**Scenario:** The prospect says: *"Yeah, I'm interested, but I'm really busy. I don't know if I'll have time to send you reports every month."*

**Rep must:**
- [ ] Use the "flying blind" analogy
- [ ] Explain the phone number as unique identifier
- [ ] Lower the barrier with the "bare minimum" technique (phone, quoted, sold — that's it)
- [ ] Overcome the sorting objection (just send the raw CRM export)
- [ ] Establish the non-negotiable boundary without being aggressive

### Exercise 2: Role-Play — The 90-Day Ask (10 min)

**Setup:** Pair reps. One plays the prospect, one plays the GOAL rep.

**Scenario:** The prospect says: *"Okay, I'll try it. But if I don't see results in the first couple weeks, I'm out."*

**Rep must:**
- [ ] Use the "no silver bullet" / "not pushing a button" language
- [ ] Explain the data maturity timeline (Month 1 = learning, Month 2 = optimizing, Month 3 = performing)
- [ ] Execute the "handshake agreement" technique (no contracts, but give us 90 days)
- [ ] Reference that the onboarding call will reinforce the same expectation

### Exercise 3: Role-Play — "Burned by Lead Vendors" (10 min)

**Setup:** Pair reps. One plays the prospect, one plays the GOAL rep.

**Scenario:** The prospect says: *"Look, I've spent thousands on leads from EverQuote and Smart Financial. They were all garbage. Why would this be any different?"*

**Rep must:**
- [ ] Validate the frustration (don't dismiss it)
- [ ] Use the "anti-lead company" rebuttal ("we're not a lead company, we're a marketing platform")
- [ ] Expose the aggregator model — explain why those leads were bad (resold 5-8x, consumer bombarded)
- [ ] Pitch the 1-to-1 model with all three differentiators (direct consent, brand equity, final destination)
- [ ] Handle the "exclusive" follow-up with the "seeing Jesus" analogy

## Part 5: Key Takeaways & Accountability — 5 minutes

### The Three Non-Negotiables Every Rep Must Set Before Closing

| # | Expectation | What to Say | Why It Matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Data is mandatory** | "We need phone number, quoted, and sold. Without it, we're flying blind." | AM cannot optimize without disposition data. Account will stagnate and churn. |
| 2 | **90 days minimum** | "Give us a 90-day window. No contracts, just a handshake." | Campaigns need data maturity. Premature judgments kill accounts. |
| 3 | **We are NOT a lead vendor** | "We're a marketing platform. You're building your own lead engine." | Prevents toxic lead-buying mindset from poisoning the AM relationship. |

### The Accountability Standard

- If an account churns within 60 days because expectations weren't set, it traces back to the sale
- Account Managers will flag accounts where the prospect was clearly not prepped on data requirements or the 90-day timeline
- The goal is **not** to scare prospects away — it's to close clients who are set up to succeed

### Chris Nashed's Final Word to the Sales Team:

> *"We're looking at that first 90-day window as the most critical time to drive performance, and that's really when they're going to make a decision of whether they're going to continue or churn. It's an opportunity to root them in reality."*
