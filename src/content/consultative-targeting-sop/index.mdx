# Consultative Campaign Targeting

Transition from order-taker to trusted marketing advisor — coach agents into campaign setups that preserve volume, improve efficiency, and scale sustainably.

---

## Overview & Learning Objectives

<TipCallout variant="info" title="Module Purpose">
This module teaches reps how to guide agents toward campaign configurations that balance **volume and quality**. The core narrative: over-filtering feels safe but destroys volume, smart pacing comes from strategy not just spend, and winning accounts use a structured approach to day-parting, bid modifiers, and geo strategy.
</TipCallout>

<InfoCard title="Learning Objectives" icon="🎯">
By the end of this module, you will be able to:

1. Diagnose when an agent is unintentionally over-filtering into low volume.
2. Explain the "volume vs. quality" tradeoff using simple, agent-friendly language.
3. Recommend day-parting windows that reduce wasted spend and avoid peak competition.
4. Propose a geo and bid-modifier strategy that balances coverage with "hot zip" focus.
5. Run a consultative discovery conversation that ends with a clear targeting plan.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Prerequisites" icon="📋">
Before starting this module, you should have:

- Basic understanding of GOAL campaign structure and bidding concepts.
- Familiarity with common KPIs (CPA, lead volume, close rate).
</InfoCard>

## Key Terms & Concepts

Master these terms before diving into the lessons. You will use them in every targeting conversation with agents.

<InfoCard title="Over-Filtering" icon="🚫">
Tight filters that reduce deliverable lead volume below what a budget can efficiently spend. Over-filtering feels like quality control, but it usually collapses volume and inflates CPA. The campaign starves for data, learning slows, and performance becomes unpredictable.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Day-Parting" icon="🕐">
Adjusting campaign run times to concentrate spend in higher-performing windows. Instead of running 24/7 and letting budget burn in competitive hours, day-parting directs spend toward times when cost-per-lead is lower and intent is higher.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Bid Modifier" icon="🎛️">
Bid adjustments by geo or segment to control where budget wins auctions. Rather than a flat bid everywhere, modifiers let you pay more in areas that close well and less in areas you are testing. Think of it as volume control by zip code.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Hot Zips" icon="📍">
Zip codes that historically close at higher rates. These are identified from the agent's own close data or from platform performance history. Hot zips get bid emphasis, but they should never be the *only* zips targeted.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Statewide Volume" icon="🌐">
Broad geographic coverage to capture lower-cost demand at scale. Statewide volume keeps the funnel fed, generates learning data, and often produces leads at a lower cost-per-acquisition than hyper-targeted campaigns.
</InfoCard>

## The Danger of Over-Filtering

**Core Lesson:** Teach agents to spot the "filter trap" and confidently coach them away from it. Over-filtering is the most common campaign mistake — and the hardest to explain without offending the agent.

<TipCallout variant="warning" title="Why This Matters">
Agents over-filter because they want "only the best" leads. But tight filters reduce volume, increase auction costs, and starve the campaign of the data it needs to learn and optimize. The result is the opposite of what they intended: worse performance, not better.
</TipCallout>

<Accordion title="The Multi-Car Myth">
Agents often request strict "multi-car only" targeting. The logic seems sound — multi-car households are higher-value policies.

**The problem:** Many high-intent shoppers will not add a second vehicle on the first form. They are still shopping. By requiring multi-car on the initial form, the campaign excludes a huge segment of shoppers who *are* multi-car households but haven't indicated it yet.

**The outcome:** The campaign starves and CPA rises. The agent gets fewer leads, pays more per lead, and blames "lead quality" — when the real issue is that the filter collapsed the audience before intent could be measured.

**The coaching point:** "Multi-car is a great outcome to optimize toward, but it's a terrible *filter* to start with. Let's capture intent first, then optimize for household size once we have data."
</Accordion>

### Why Agents Over-Filter

There are three common reasons, and each one requires a different coaching response:

1. **They want "only the best" leads** — They are trying to pre-qualify at the filter level instead of the conversation level.
2. **They are managing call center workload** — They think fewer leads means less work, but it actually means less *predictable* work.
3. **Bad experience with other lead vendors** — They got burned by shared or recycled leads and now try to control quality through volume restriction.

### Mini-Diagnosis Checklist

Use this checklist when you suspect an agent is over-filtered. If two or more items are true, the campaign is likely filter-constrained.

<ChecklistItem id="ct-diag-1">Budget is not spending fully or consistently</ChecklistItem>
<ChecklistItem id="ct-diag-2">Lead volume is inconsistent day to day</ChecklistItem>
<ChecklistItem id="ct-diag-3">Campaign has too many required attributes (multi-car + homeowner + specific zips + carrier targeting)</ChecklistItem>
<ChecklistItem id="ct-diag-4">Agent describes their "ideal lead" in a way that collapses the addressable audience</ChecklistItem>

## Advanced Day-Parting Tactics

**Core Lesson:** Position day-parting as a way to protect budget and concentrate spend where performance is strongest — not as "spending less."

<TipCallout variant="info" title="The Key Insight">
Day-parting is not about reducing spend. It is about redirecting spend away from expensive, competitive windows and toward hours where cost-per-lead is lower and shopper intent is higher. The agent's total budget stays the same — it just works harder.
</TipCallout>

### The Competition Problem

Heavy competition in morning rush windows (typically 8:00 AM - 10:00 AM) can burn through budget fast. Every lead vendor and carrier is bidding aggressively in these hours, which drives up cost-per-lead without necessarily improving quality.

The "golden window" is often the afternoon (4:00 PM - 6:00 PM) when working shoppers are actively looking but competition has thinned out.

### Day-Parting Framework

<StepByStep steps={[
  {
    title: "Step 1: Start with a Baseline Schedule",
    content: "Run broad hours for the first 1-2 weeks to collect data. Don't make assumptions about which hours perform best — let the data tell you. A baseline schedule typically runs 7 AM - 9 PM local time."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 2: Identify Budget Burn Windows",
    content: "Review hourly performance data to find windows where CPA spikes or where budget burns fast without proportional lead quality. Morning rush (8-10 AM) is the most common burn window, but it varies by market."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 3: Shift Spend to Higher-Intent Windows",
    content: "Reduce or pause spend during burn windows and redirect budget toward windows with better cost efficiency. Afternoon and early evening often produce better results because competition is lower and shoppers are more deliberate."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 4: Re-Check After Sufficient Volume",
    content: "After 50-100 leads under the new schedule, review again. Day-parting is iterative — the first adjustment won't be perfect. Look for new patterns and refine. Don't change the schedule more than once per week."
  }
]} />

## Bid Modifiers & Geographic Strategy

**Core Lesson:** Teach agents how to propose a geo strategy that captures affordable volume while prioritizing high-closing areas — without betting everything on a tiny pocket of demand.

<TipCallout variant="info" title="The Hybrid Approach">
The best geo strategy is almost never "only my best zips." It is a hybrid: broad coverage to keep the funnel fed, with bid modifiers to emphasize areas where close rates are strongest. This gives you volume *and* quality.
</TipCallout>

### The Hybrid Geo Strategy

<StepByStep steps={[
  {
    title: "Step 1: Start Statewide or Broad Coverage",
    content: "Run the campaign across the full service area. This keeps volume healthy, generates data across geographies, and often produces leads at a lower cost than hyper-targeted campaigns. Broad coverage is the foundation."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 2: Identify 10-30 Hot Zips",
    content: "Work with the agent to identify zip codes that historically close at higher rates. Sources: agent's own close data, platform performance history, or market knowledge. These become your emphasis zones — not your only zones."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 3: Apply Bid Modifiers",
    content: "Increase bids in hot zips to win more auctions in those areas. Keep base bids running statewide so you don't lose the volume foundation. The modifier should be meaningful but not extreme — a 20-40% boost is a good starting point."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 4: Review and Adjust",
    content: "After 2-4 weeks, review performance by geography. Some hot zips may underperform expectations; some 'cold' areas may surprise you. Adjust modifiers based on actual data, not assumptions. Geo strategy is iterative, not permanent."
  }
]} />

<Accordion title="Common Mistakes in Geo Strategy">
These are the three most frequent errors reps and agents make with geographic targeting:

1. **Only targeting hot zips and starving volume.** This is the geo equivalent of over-filtering. A campaign that only runs in 5-10 zip codes has a tiny addressable audience and will struggle to spend budget consistently.

2. **Making too many simultaneous changes.** Agents who change geo targeting, day-parting, and bid modifiers all at once can't tell what's working. Change one variable at a time and measure the impact before adjusting another.

3. **Treating geo as permanent instead of iterative.** Markets shift. Zip code performance changes seasonally. A geo strategy that worked in Q1 may need adjustment in Q3. Build in regular review cycles.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Carrier Targeting: Proceed with Caution">
When agents ask about targeting shoppers of specific carriers, coach them to treat it as a **hypothesis, not a guarantee.**

- Carrier-shopping behavior is harder to capture on forms than agents expect.
- A controlled test (specific carrier filter on a subset of budget) is better than a sweeping change.
- Frame it as: "Let's test it with 20% of your budget for 2 weeks and see if the data supports scaling it up."
- Never promise that carrier targeting will improve results — it may or may not, and the only way to know is to test.
</Accordion>

## Consultative Discovery Flow

Before giving any targeting recommendations, run this discovery flow. It ensures your recommendations are grounded in the agent's actual situation — not assumptions.

<TipCallout variant="tip" title="Why Discovery First">
Reps who skip discovery and jump straight to recommendations sound like order-takers, not advisors. The 5 minutes you spend on discovery will save you from recommending a setup the agent doesn't trust or can't execute.
</TipCallout>

<StepByStep steps={[
  {
    title: "Phase 1: Agent Goals",
    content: "Understand what success looks like.\n\n- What's your growth target? (leads per week, policies per month)\n- What timeline are you working with?\n- What's an acceptable CPA range?\n\nListen for unrealistic expectations early — this is where you calibrate."
  },
  {
    title: "Phase 2: Capacity",
    content: "Understand what they can actually handle.\n\n- How fast can you or your team call leads? (speed-to-lead)\n- What's your staffing situation?\n- What are your best contact hours?\n\nIf an agent wants 50 leads/day but has one person calling, the targeting plan needs to account for that."
  },
  {
    title: "Phase 3: Market",
    content: "Understand the competitive landscape.\n\n- What's your service area?\n- How competitive is your market?\n- Any seasonal patterns?\n\nThis informs geo strategy and day-parting. A saturated urban market behaves differently than a rural one."
  },
  {
    title: "Phase 4: Constraints",
    content: "Understand what's off the table.\n\n- Any compliance requirements?\n- Brand restrictions from the carrier?\n- Budget floor or ceiling?\n\nConstraints aren't objections — they're guardrails that make your recommendation more credible."
  },
  {
    title: "Phase 5: Plan",
    content: "Synthesize into a clear next step.\n\n- Baseline setup (filters, geo, schedule)\n- Early learning period (2 weeks of data collection)\n- Optimization cycle (review, adjust, repeat)\n\nEnd with: 'Here's what I recommend we start with, and here's when we'll review and adjust.'"
  }
]} />

<ChecklistItem id="ct-disc-1">I ask about agent goals (growth target, timeline, CPA range) before recommending a setup</ChecklistItem>
<ChecklistItem id="ct-disc-2">I confirm capacity (speed-to-lead, staffing, contact hours) to size the campaign correctly</ChecklistItem>
<ChecklistItem id="ct-disc-3">I gather market context (service area, competition, seasonality) to inform geo and day-parting</ChecklistItem>
<ChecklistItem id="ct-disc-4">I identify constraints (compliance, brand, budget) to make the recommendation credible</ChecklistItem>
<ChecklistItem id="ct-disc-5">I synthesize discovery into a baseline setup, learning period, and optimization cycle</ChecklistItem>
