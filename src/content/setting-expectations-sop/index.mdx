# Setting Expectations & Handling Objections

Equip reps to set non-negotiable expectations during the sales process so the Account Management team can succeed post-sale.

---

## Pre-Training Context

<TipCallout variant="info" title="Purpose">
This training exists because successful GOAL accounts share one thing in common: the rep set strict boundaries during the sale. Failed accounts almost always trace back to expectations that were never set or were softened to close the deal.
</TipCallout>

<InfoCard title="Why This Training Exists" icon="⚠️">
As Chris Nashed warns: *"It's not going to be surprising to me if some sellers who feel like they can get somebody over the finish line... are going to talk sunshine, rainbows, and lollipops."* The cost of a bad close: Account Managers become the "bearer of bad news," trust erodes, and the account churns — costing everyone revenue.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="The Three Situations Covered" icon="📋">
This training covers the three most critical expectations every rep must set before closing: **(1)** The data requirement — disposition data is mandatory, **(2)** The 90-day ramp — campaigns need time to mature, and **(3)** The "burned by lead vendors" objection — reframing GOAL away from the lead vendor mindset.
</InfoCard>

---

## Part 1: The Data Requirement

**Core Principle:** GOAL is a data-driven platform. Without backend disposition data, the Account Manager cannot optimize campaigns, prove ROI, or lower the client's CPA. Providing this data is **not optional** — it is a requirement of partnership.

<StepByStep steps={[
  {
    title: "Step 1: Introduce the Data Requirement",
    content: "Frame it as being in the prospect's best interest using the 'flying blind' analogy: 'Everything we do here is a data-driven decision. In order for me to send you better leads, I have to know who you contact, who you quote, and who you sell. Without it, I'm flying completely blind.'"
  },
  {
    title: "Step 2: Lower the Barrier",
    content: "Use the 'bare minimum' technique to reduce friction: 'All we really care about is the phone number. I don't care what their name is or address. I need phone number, I need to know if you quoted it and if you sold it. That's it.'"
  },
  {
    title: "Step 3: Explain the Mechanics",
    content: "Walk them through how the phone number works as a unique identifier: 'We use the phone number as a unique identifier for the consumer. Whether you quoted it, whether you sold it — I can map that in. Then I can tell you by ad group: how many quotes, what's my quote conversion rate from a click, cost per quote, how many households did I sell.'"
  },
  {
    title: "Step 4: Kill the Sorting Objection",
    content: "Proactively address the assumption they need to filter their CRM: 'Most agents just send us the raw export from their CRM with all their stuff. They don't even filter by vendors. Our system is smart enough to map things based on the phone number. If it didn't originate from GOAL, it doesn't count.'"
  }
]} />

## Part 2: The 90-Day Ramp Conversation

**Core Principle:** GOAL is not an overnight solution. Digital marketing campaigns require data to mature, algorithms to optimize, and sales cycles to complete. Reps must secure a verbal 90-day commitment from every prospect before closing.

<Accordion title="Validated Example: The Handshake in Action">
**Mayra Menchaca's demo:** The rep said: *"No contracts... We asked for three months at a minimum. It's informal. It's like a handshake agreement. I don't need to make you sign something. It's just kind of like, give me three months."*

The prospect's response: *"One month is not enough data. It's just not enough."*

**Result:** The prospect voluntarily agreed because the logic was sound. When you explain the "why" behind 90 days, prospects buy into it naturally.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Case Study: The Kansas City Account — Premature Churn">
A Kansas City agent launched with a $25/day budget. On day one, he received one inbound call, quoted it, and was thrilled. When volume naturally dropped the following week (because $25/day yields minimal volume), his mindset shifted entirely.

Alex's account: *"He churned like in two weeks. I think it was 11 days or 13 days... he gave us seven or nine business days and saw some results but not enough to continue."*

Alex's reflection: *"I feel like maybe I could have said like $25 a day is going to get you very little results... if you double down on the 90-day thing... you're going to get people like that."*

**Takeaway:** If the rep doesn't explicitly secure the 90-day commitment, low-budget clients will judge the platform on a few days of data and churn before the AM has any chance to optimize.
</Accordion>

## Part 3: Handling the "Burned by Lead Vendors" Objection

**Core Principle:** This is the single most common objection reps will face. Nearly every insurance agent has spent money on shared leads from aggregators and had a terrible experience. Reps must **validate the frustration**, **explain why the experience was bad**, and **reframe GOAL as a fundamentally different model**.

<StepByStep steps={[
  {
    title: "Step 1: Validate the Frustration",
    content: "Never dismiss the prospect's bad experience. Acknowledge it: 'I completely understand that frustration. You're not alone — nearly every agent we talk to has had a similar experience with shared leads.'"
  },
  {
    title: "Step 2: Use the Anti-Lead Company Rebuttal",
    content: "Immediately separate GOAL from the comparison: 'I don't want you to think of us as another lead company because we're not a lead company. We're a marketing platform. You're cutting out the middleman and advertising on the same networks as Progressive, Geico, and EverQuote.'"
  },
  {
    title: "Step 3: Expose the Aggregator Model",
    content: "Explain WHY their past experience was bad: Consumer fills out one form → aggregator sells that data 3–8+ times → consumer gets bombarded → by the time Agent #4 calls, they're annoyed or already quoted → agent blames 'the leads' when the real problem is the model."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 4: Pitch the 1-to-1 Model",
    content: "Contrast with GOAL's three differentiators: (1) Direct Consent — collecting consent 1-to-1, not reselling data, (2) Brand Equity — consumers interact with YOUR brand, not a generic marketplace, (3) Final Destination — the only call to action is to call YOU."
  },
  {
    title: "Step 5: Handle the 'Exclusive' Follow-Up",
    content: "Use radical transparency: 'Anyone that tells you you're going to get exclusive leads in the lead gen game is lying. No one can control human behavior. But GOAL is the closest thing to exclusivity because the data is never resold by GOAL.'"
  }
]} />

<Accordion title="Case Study: The 'Lead Vendor Mindset' Client">
An account came in treating GOAL like a generic lead vendor. He complained about individual leads, demanded constant adjustments, but refused to ever get on a call with his Account Manager.

Tom's account: *"He refuses to meet. He refuses to ever meet with Nick. He's never met with anyone. He'll only do it over text."*

To appease the complaints, Nick began manually removing publisher sources entirely instead of properly filtering — which Chris flagged as a destructive approach to campaign management.

Tom's conclusion: *"If you don't ever want to meet or focus on optimizing this, maybe we're not the right product for you."*

**Takeaway:** When the rep fails to reframe GOAL away from "lead vendor," the client brings toxic lead-buying habits into the relationship and resists the collaborative partnership model GOAL requires.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Handling the 'Exclusive' Question with Radical Transparency">
Prospects will ask: "Are these exclusive leads?" This is a trap. Never promise exclusivity — instead, use radical transparency to build trust.

*"Exclusive in this space — you've got a better chance of seeing Jesus than generating real, true exclusivity. And what I mean by that is we can't control human behavior."*

*"Anyone that tells you you're going to get exclusive in the lead gen game is lying through their freaking teeth, and you should fire them immediately. Because that means they're saying that they can control human behavior."*

**Then pivot:** While no one can guarantee a consumer won't fill out multiple forms, GOAL is the closest thing to exclusivity because the data is never resold by GOAL. The consumer comes through YOUR branded QuoteFunnel — you're the only agent they see.
</Accordion>

## Part 4: Interactive Exercises

### Exercise 1: Setting the Data Requirement

<Accordion title="Role-Play: The Busy Prospect">
**Scenario:** The prospect says: *"Yeah, I'm interested, but I'm really busy. I don't know if I'll have time to send you reports every month."*

**You must demonstrate:**
- Use the "flying blind" analogy
- Explain the phone number as unique identifier
- Lower the barrier with the "bare minimum" technique (phone, quoted, sold — that's it)
- Overcome the sorting objection (just send the raw CRM export)
- Establish the non-negotiable boundary without being aggressive
</Accordion>

### Exercise 2: The 90-Day Ask

<Accordion title="Role-Play: The Impatient Prospect">
**Scenario:** The prospect says: *"Okay, I'll try it. But if I don't see results in the first couple weeks, I'm out."*

**You must demonstrate:**
- Use the "no silver bullet" / "not pushing a button" language
- Explain the data maturity timeline (Month 1 = learning, Month 2 = optimizing, Month 3 = performing)
- Execute the "handshake agreement" technique (no contracts, but give us 90 days)
- Reference that the onboarding call will reinforce the same expectation
</Accordion>

### Exercise 3: "Burned by Lead Vendors"

<Accordion title="Role-Play: The Frustrated Buyer">
**Scenario:** The prospect says: *"Look, I've spent thousands on leads from EverQuote and Smart Financial. They were all garbage. Why would this be any different?"*

**You must demonstrate:**
- Validate the frustration (don't dismiss it)
- Use the "anti-lead company" rebuttal ("we're not a lead company, we're a marketing platform")
- Expose the aggregator model — explain why those leads were bad (resold 5–8x, consumer bombarded)
- Pitch the 1-to-1 model with all three differentiators (direct consent, brand equity, final destination)
- Handle the "exclusive" follow-up with the "seeing Jesus" analogy
</Accordion>

## Part 5: Key Takeaways

<InfoCard title="1. Data is Mandatory" icon="📊">
"We need phone number, quoted, and sold. Without it, we're flying blind." The AM cannot optimize without disposition data. The account will stagnate and churn.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="2. 90 Days Minimum" icon="📅">
"Give us a 90-day window. No contracts, just a handshake." Campaigns need data maturity. Premature judgments kill accounts.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="3. We Are NOT a Lead Vendor" icon="🚫">
"We're a marketing platform. You're building your own lead engine." This prevents the toxic lead-buying mindset from poisoning the AM relationship.
</InfoCard>

<TipCallout variant="important" title="The Accountability Standard">
If an account churns within 60 days because expectations weren't set, it traces back to the sale. Account Managers will flag accounts where the prospect was clearly not prepped. The goal is not to scare prospects away — it's to close clients who are set up to succeed.
</TipCallout>
