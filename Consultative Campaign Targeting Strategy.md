# Consultative Campaign Targeting Strategy

- **Module:** Consultative Campaign Targeting Strategy
- **Audience:** GOAL Sales Reps and Account Managers
- **Outcome:** Transition from “order-taker” to trusted marketing advisor by coaching agents into campaign setups that preserve volume, improve efficiency, and scale sustainably.

This module teaches reps how to guide agents toward campaign configurations that balance **volume and quality**. The core narrative is:
- Over-filtering feels safe, but it often destroys volume and inflates CPA.
- Smart pacing comes from **strategy**, not just spend.
- Winning accounts use a structured approach to **day-parting**, **bid modifiers**, and **geo strategy**.

## Learning objectives

By the end, learners can:
- Diagnose when an agent is unintentionally over-filtering into low volume.
- Explain the “volume vs. quality” tradeoff using simple, agent-friendly language.
- Recommend day-parting windows that reduce wasted spend and avoid peak competition.
- Propose a geo and bid-modifier strategy that balances coverage with “hot zip” focus.
- Run a consultative discovery conversation that ends with a clear targeting plan.

## Prerequisites

- Basic understanding of GOAL campaign structure and bidding concepts.
- Familiarity with common KPIs (CPA, lead volume, close rate).

## Key terms (use consistently)

- **Over-filtering:** Tight filters that reduce deliverable lead volume below what a budget can efficiently spend.
- **Day-parting:** Adjusting campaign run times to concentrate spend in higher-performing windows.
- **Bid modifier:** Bid adjustments by geo or segment to control where budget wins auctions.
- **Hot zips:** Zip codes that historically close at higher rates.
- **Statewide volume:** Broad coverage to capture lower-cost demand at scale.

## Recommended delivery format

- **Total time:** 45–60 minutes
- **Structure:** 3 lessons + case study workshop + roleplay + knowledge check
- **Assets:** Example “over-filtered vs. balanced” setups, sample day-parting schedules, geo maps/zip lists (if available)

# Lesson 2.1 — The danger of over-filtering

**Duration:** 15–20 minutes

### Teaching goal

Teach learners to spot the “filter trap” and confidently coach agents away from it.

### Key points to cover

- **Why agents over-filter**
  - They want “only the best” leads.
  - They are trying to control call center workload.
  - They had a bad experience with shared/recycled leads elsewhere.
- **What over-filtering causes**
  - Lower volume.
  - Higher auction costs.
  - Less data, slower learning, and unstable performance.

### The multi-car myth (example)

- Agents often request strict “multi-car only” targeting.
- The problem: many high-intent shoppers will not add a second vehicle on the first form.
- Outcome: the campaign starves and CPA rises.

### Talk track (coachable)

- “Filters are like a valve. If we close it too far, we do not get better water. We just get less water.”
- “We can improve quality *after* we have stable volume. First we build the pipeline, then we tune it.”

### Mini-diagnosis checklist

- Is the budget not spending?
- Are leads inconsistent day to day?
- Are there too many required attributes?
- Is the agent describing their ideal lead in a way that collapses the audience?

### Learner activity (5 minutes)

- Give learners 3 “agent requests.”
  - Example: “Only multi-car, only homeowners, only [zip list], only [carrier] shoppers.”
- Ask: What is your consultative response, and what would you loosen first?

### Knowledge check

1. What are two symptoms of over-filtering?
2. Why can “multi-car only” reduce performance?

# Lesson 2.2 — Advanced day-parting tactics

**Duration:** 15–20 minutes

### Teaching goal

Teach learners to position day-parting as a way to protect budget and concentrate spend where performance is strongest.

### Key points to cover

- **Avoiding the bloodbath**
  - Heavy competition in morning rush windows (example: 8:00 AM–10:00 AM) can burn budget.
- **The golden window**
  - Position an afternoon window (example: 4:00 PM–6:00 PM) as a time to catch working shoppers.

### How to recommend day-parting (framework)

1. Start with a baseline schedule.
2. Identify budget burn windows.
3. Shift spend toward higher-intent or better-cost windows.
4. Re-check after enough volume.

### Talk track (coachable)

- “We are not spending less. We are spending smarter. We want your budget fighting in winnable auctions.”
- “If we push spend into the most competitive hours, we pay the premium without guaranteed quality.”

### Practical templates

- **Starter schedule:** run broad hours, then refine.
- **Defensive schedule:** exclude known “burn” windows.
- **Scaling schedule:** expand hours once stable.

### Learner activity (5 minutes)

- Provide a simple scenario:
  - “Budget burns early, CPA spikes, volume falls off midday.”
- Ask learners to propose a day-parting adjustment in 3 bullets.

### Knowledge check

1. What is the goal of day-parting?
2. What is one reason morning rush can be inefficient?

# Lesson 2.3 — Bid modifiers and geographic strategy

**Duration:** 15–20 minutes

### Teaching goal

Teach learners how to propose a geo strategy that captures cheap volume while still prioritizing high-closing areas.

### Core strategy: statewide + hot zips (hybrid)

- Run broader coverage to keep the funnel fed.
- Boost bids where close rates are historically stronger.

### How to explain it simply

- “We use the whole state to keep volume healthy, and we add emphasis where you win the most.”
- “We do not want to bet everything on a tiny pocket of demand.”

### Step-by-step recommendation

1. Start statewide or broad coverage.
2. Identify 10–30 “hot zips” (based on close history or agent intel).
3. Apply bid modifiers to prioritize those areas.
4. Review performance and adjust.

### Carrier targeting (when relevant)

- Teach learners how to frame demographic or competitor targeting as a hypothesis, not a guarantee.
- Emphasize controlled tests, not sweeping changes.

### Common mistakes

- Only targeting hot zips and starving volume.
- Making too many simultaneous changes.
- Treating geo as permanent instead of iterative.

### Learner activity (5–7 minutes)

- Case study:
  - “Agent wants only 5 zips because those are ‘their best’.”
- Learners respond with:
  - A consultative reframing.
  - A hybrid plan.
  - One risk to watch.

## Consultative discovery flow (rep-ready)

Use this flow before giving recommendations:
1. **Agent goals:** growth target, timeline, acceptable CPA range.
2. **Capacity:** how fast they can call, staffing, best contact hours.
3. **Market:** service areas, competitive pressure, seasonality.
4. **Constraints:** compliance, brand restrictions, carrier considerations.
5. **Plan:** baseline setup → early learning → optimization cycle.

## Module deliverables

Learners should leave with:
- A repeatable way to explain over-filtering without offending the agent.
- A day-parting recommendation template.
- A geo strategy that uses statewide volume plus hot-zip emphasis.
- A short “targeting plan” they can summarize in 60 seconds.

## Assessment (pass/fail rubric)

Pass if the learner can:
- Diagnose an over-filtered setup and propose what to loosen first.
- Recommend a day-parting change with a clear rationale.
- Propose a hybrid geo strategy and explain why it works.
- Roleplay a consultative conversation that ends with a plan.

## Coaching notes for managers

- Coach for clarity and confidence.
- Reinforce that “less filtering” is not “lower quality.” It is “more learnings + stable volume.”
- Watch for reps making guarantees.

## Appendix — Copy-ready talk tracks

### 30-second pitch

“Most targeting problems come from over-filtering. It feels like quality control, but it usually collapses volume and drives CPA up. The better approach is a consultative setup: keep volume healthy, then use day-parting and geo bid modifiers to concentrate spend where performance is strongest. That gives you stable lead flow and a clear path to optimization.”

### 10-second definition

“Consultative targeting is keeping the funnel wide enough to learn, then using schedule and geo levers to improve efficiency.”
