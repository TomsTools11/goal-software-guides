## Key Terminology

Before diving into procedures, get familiar with the core vocabulary you will encounter daily in Close CRM. Expand each card below for a concise definition.

<TipCallout variant="info" title="Why Terminology Matters">
Close CRM uses specific terms that map directly to your sales workflow. Understanding these upfront prevents confusion when configuring pipelines, building Smart Views, or discussing pipeline metrics with your team.
</TipCallout>

<InfoCard title="Lead" icon="🏢">
A **Lead** is the top-level entity in Close. It represents a company or organization you are pursuing. A single Lead can contain multiple Contacts, Opportunities, and Activities. Think of it as the "account" container.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Contact" icon="👤">
A **Contact** is an individual person associated with a Lead. Each Contact has their own email, phone number, and communication history. A Lead can have many Contacts — for example, the CEO, CTO, and purchasing manager at the same company.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Opportunity" icon="💰">
An **Opportunity** represents a potential deal tied to a Lead. It has a monetary value, a confidence percentage, and an expected close date. Opportunities move through Pipeline stages as the deal progresses.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Pipeline" icon="🔄">
A **Pipeline** is a visual representation of your sales stages — from initial qualification to closed-won or closed-lost. Each Pipeline contains ordered stages (e.g., Qualification → Proposal → Negotiation → Closed Won). You can create multiple Pipelines for different sales processes.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Smart View" icon="🔍">
A **Smart View** is a saved, dynamic filter that surfaces specific subsets of your Leads. For example, "Leads with no activity in 7 days" or "High-value opportunities closing this month." Smart Views update automatically as data changes.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Activity" icon="📋">
An **Activity** is any interaction logged against a Lead — emails sent, calls made, SMS messages, notes, or meetings. Activities create the communication timeline that powers reporting and Smart Views.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Custom Field" icon="🏷️">
A **Custom Field** is a user-defined data point you add to Leads, Contacts, or Opportunities. Examples include "Lead Source," "Industry," or "Contract Renewal Date." Custom Fields allow you to segment, filter, and report on data specific to your business.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Lead Status" icon="🚦">
A **Lead Status** categorizes where a Lead sits in your overall sales process (e.g., Potential, Qualified, Unqualified, Customer). This is different from Opportunity stages — Lead Status tracks the *relationship*, while Pipeline stages track the *deal*.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Task" icon="✅">
A **Task** is a to-do item assigned to a team member, optionally linked to a Lead. Tasks have due dates and can trigger reminders. Use them for follow-ups, proposal deadlines, and accountability checkpoints.
</InfoCard>

<InfoCard title="Workflow" icon="⚡">
A **Workflow** is an automated sequence of actions triggered by events in Close. For example, when a Lead Status changes to "Qualified," a Workflow can automatically create a Task, send an email template, or update a Custom Field.
</InfoCard>

Before your team begins using Close CRM, the environment must be configured correctly. A poorly set up CRM leads to inconsistent data, missed follow-ups, and unreliable reporting.

  { title: "Create Custom Fields", content: "Navigate to Settings → Custom Fields. Do not skip Custom Field configuration. If your team starts entering Leads before Custom Fields exist, you will end up with inconsistent data that is painful to clean up later. Always finish environment setup *before* importing or creating Leads.
</TipCallout>

## Procedure 3: Managing Opportunities & Pipeline

Once a Lead is qualified, you convert interest into a trackable Opportunity. The Pipeline view gives your team a real-time snapshot of deal flow, revenue projections, and bottlenecks.

  src="/images/guides/close-crm/pipeline.png"

  title="Close CRM — Sales Pipeline"
  caption="The Pipeline board — drag opportunities between stages, review deal values, and spot stalled deals at a glance."
/>

<StepByStep steps={[
  { title: "Create an Opportunity", content: "Open the Lead and click 'Add Opportunity.' Enter the deal value (estimated contract amount), select the Pipeline, set the current stage (usually Qualification), and add an expected close date. Set the confidence percentage — this affects your weighted pipeline forecast." },
  { title: "Move deals through stages", content: "As the deal progresses, update the Opportunity stage. After a successful discovery call, move from Qualification to Discovery. After sending a proposal, move to Proposal. Each stage change should be accompanied by an Activity log explaining what happened and what the next step is." },
  { title: "Update deal values and dates", content: "As you learn more about the deal, update the Opportunity value and expected close date. A $50K estimate that becomes a $35K proposal should be adjusted immediately — your pipeline forecast depends on accurate numbers, not optimistic guesses." },
  { title: "Use the Pipeline board view", content: "Switch to the Pipeline board view for a visual Kanban-style layout. You can drag Opportunities between stages. Pay attention to deals that have been stuck in the same stage for too long — these are the ones most likely to go stale. Use Smart Views to surface stalled deals automatically." },
  { title: "Close the deal", content: "When a deal is won, move it to Closed Won and update the final value. When a deal is lost, move it to Closed Lost and log a note explaining why — 'Lost to competitor,' 'Budget cut,' 'No response.' This loss data is critical for improving your sales process over time." }
]} />

<TipCallout variant="important" title="Pipeline Hygiene Rule">
Review your pipeline at least once per week. Delete or close Opportunities that are clearly dead. A bloated pipeline with stale deals gives leadership a false sense of revenue forecasting and hides real problems. Your weighted pipeline value should reflect reality, not hope.
</TipCallout>

## Critical Nuances

These are the non-obvious details that separate productive CRM usage from frustrating, data-polluted workflows. Each nuance below comes from real-world experience managing Close CRM at scale.

<TipCallout variant="warning" title="Data Integrity Is Non-Negotiable">
A CRM is only as valuable as the data it contains. If your team treats Close as an afterthought — logging activities days late, leaving Custom Fields blank, or letting Opportunities stagnate — the entire system becomes unreliable. Make CRM hygiene a daily habit, not a weekly chore.
</TipCallout>

<Accordion title="Lead vs. Opportunity: Understanding the Distinction">
New Close users often confuse Leads and Opportunities. Here is the rule: a **Lead** is the *company relationship* — it exists regardless of whether there is an active deal. An **Opportunity** is a *specific deal* being pursued with that company. One Lead can have multiple Opportunities over time (e.g., an initial contract, a renewal, an upsell). When you close a deal, the Opportunity is closed but the Lead remains active as a customer. Never delete Leads when deals close — update the Lead Status to "Customer" instead.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Avoiding Duplicate Records">
Duplicates are the most common data quality issue in any CRM. Before creating a new Lead, always search Close by company name, domain, and contact email. Close has a built-in duplicate detection warning, but it is not foolproof. If you discover duplicates, merge them immediately via the Lead detail page (Actions → Merge). Unresolved duplicates lead to split communication histories and inaccurate reporting.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Smart View Best Practices">
Smart Views are powerful but can become unwieldy. Follow these rules: (1) Name them clearly — "Hot Leads No Activity 7d" is better than "My Filter." (2) Keep filters specific — a view with 500+ results is too broad to be actionable. (3) Share team-critical views with the entire organization. (4) Review and prune Smart Views monthly — delete views that nobody uses.
</Accordion>

<Accordion title="Email Sequences and Automation Pitfalls">
Close supports email sequences (automated follow-up chains). Use them for initial outreach to cold leads, but **never** for active deal communication. Automated emails in the middle of a negotiation feel impersonal and can damage trust. Additionally, always set sequence sending limits — blasting 500 emails in an hour will trigger spam filters and hurt your domain reputation. Start with 50–75 per day and increase gradually.
</Accordion>
